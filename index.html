Cybersecurity



What does it do? (600 words) What is the state of the art of this new technology? What can be done now? What is likely to be able to do be done soon (say in the next 3 years)? What technological or other developments make this possible?


There are a vast number of jobs in computer science and IT, from web development and software engineering to database administration and hardware engineering - no matter where your expertise lies, you are bound to be useful in some way to the tech world. Amongst the plethora of roles in CS/IT, Cybersecurity is a field that seems to be growing at an extremely fast rate in comparison to the other fields. The Bureau of Labor Statistics states that a 37% increase in the number of jobs in cybersecurity is expected for the period of 2012 to 2022. With new technology comes more efficient information processing. This means that cyber attacks are going to become more threatening as time goes on and as such; more experts in cybersecurity will be required to protect information.

Information is one of if not our most valuable resource, and the field of cybersecurity is what safeguards information. Even though we typically only hear about security breaches in large organisations (like Sony or Ashley Madison, for example), the truth is that no-one is completely safe. There is no such system that is 100% immune from an attack. While many systems may be 'computationally infeasible' to breach, it is certainly not impossible and because of this, there is an ever-present risk to important information that, if in the wrong hands, could be devastating.

Cryptography makes up a large part of cybersecurity. One example of an encryption algorithm in the public domain that is used today is the RSA encryption algorithm. This algorithm was developed by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir and Len Adleman in 1977 and is the most used cryptography algorithm used across the world.

The genius of this algorithm is grounded in 2 aspects. The first is that it uses 2 different keys, a public key (used to encrypt plaintext to get ciphertext) and a private key (used to decrypt the ciphertext to get the plaintext again). The public key is known to everyone, but the private key is known only to the receiver of the data/message. Anyone can use the public key to encrypt data, but only the holder of the private key can unravel the message. This means that the public key can be sent between parties without the plaintext message being exposed while the private key needn't go any anywhere.

The second aspect is that the algorithm uses prime numbers to make it too difficult, even for a computer, to find the private key (and therefore gain access to the secret data). Both the public key and private key uses two prime numbers (p and q). An element of the public key, n, is the product of p and q, however, p and q themselves are secret and are the 'heart', so to speak, of the RSA algorithm. If p and q are known, then an attacker can use these to calculate the private key. However, if they are not known, then an attacker can use the public key (n) to find p and q. Or can they? If p and q were not prime numbers, then there are perhaps several values that would work for p and q to get n. But since an attacker only knows n, they have to factorise n so as to get p and q that are both prime. This means that p and q can only ever have 1 possible value respectively. In an attempt to brute force p and q, the computer must do many, many calculations. If p and q are small numbers it doesn't take long, but in the real world very large numbers are used and it would take a computer far too long to find p and q which means that data encrypted by RSA is considered 'safe'.

Quantum computing is a technological advancement that is yet to reach us but is being worked on by computer scientists. It is perhaps not so close to being available, however, once the technology arrives it may prove to be problematic for modern cryptography. Rather than being based on a typical electronic system, quantum computers will be based on quantum physics. This basically means that a small unit like a bit, instead of being equal to 0 OR 1 (like in typical computers), can exist in what is called a superposition and can simultaneously be both 0 AND 1. These kinds of bits are known as qubits.

What is the likely impact? (300 words) What is the potential impact of this development? What is likely to change? Which people will be most affected and how? Will this create, replace or make redundant any current jobs or technologies?


Quantum computers technically do currently exist, but on such a small scale that they are not useful yet. IBM currently has a quantum computer that has a mere 16 qubits. To put that in perspective, typical computers can have multiple gigabytes of random access memory (RAM). This is equal to billions of regular bits. Algorithms in modern cryptography, like RSA, are considered virtually uncrackable by the computational power currently available. This is due to the use of prime factorisation briefly described above. Keys that contain a product of p and q that are hundreds of decimal digits long take so long to crack that it is pointless to try. With quantum computing, a number this large could be cracked in a matter of hours using Shor's algorithm, however quantum computers are still quite some way from being able to calculate large numbers.

In the future, stronger encryption systems will need to be devised as quantum computing will be able to break modern encryptions in a very short amount of time. The worry is that this kind of technological development will render all communication insecure as if everything was sent in plaintext. This would affect every communications system in the world and would be devastating. It is important that plans are made for replacement algorithm(s) as they could take a long time to implement. The NIST is already testing what is called 'post-quantum cryptography' which currently involves 69 new methods that could replace modern cryptography when quantum computing becomes the norm.

Machine Learning



What does it do? (600 words) What is the state of the art of this new technology? What can be done now? What is likely to be able to do be done soon (say in the next 3 years)? What technological or other developments make this possible?


Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence which is the study of algorithms in mathematics and computer science with the purpose of carrying out tasks based on conditions, rather than pre-defined instructions. Machine learning is essentially about showing a computer how to make predictions when given data. Machine learning is all about getting a computer to make sense of the messy real world. It is responsible for tasks such as separating relevant emails from spam, detecting obstacles in a self-driving vehicle as to avoid them or generating subtitles/captions for videos by analysing speech.

A state of the art example of machine learning is the AlphaGo project, created by Google's DeepMind. AlphaGo is a computer program that was developed to play Go, a complex Chinese board game (like chess), as a sort of test to see how well DeepMind's neural network algorithm could navigate a complex game like Go against not just human players, but some of the world's best. The AlphaGo project began in 2014 and operates using an algorithm that is based on machine learning techniques and 'tree search' that has been sharpened through training with other computers as well as human players. To begin, AlphaGo was given a large amount of data that consisted of moves from past Go matches between human players. The database consisted of around 30 million moves. This allowed it to mimic human play immediately. Once the AI had improved enough, AlphaGo was made to play against versions of itself and from this, it learned even more about how to play Go optimally.

In October 2015, AlphaGo beat a professional Go player for the first time ever, Fan Hui. Just months later, AlphaGo played 5 games against one of the top ranked players in the world, Lee Sedol. AlphaGo won 4 out of the 5 games they played. This was huge for the world of AI and machine learning as AlphaGo wasn't initially designed to be proficient at Go, but rather was designed to learn and was given the task of learning Go. Being the best at a board game doesn't sound like much of an achievement, but it was more of a test for the learning capabilities built into the algorithms designed by DeepMind. This means that an AI like AlphaGo can likely learn other, more practical skills that can help us in more profound ways.

There is a fear that development in machine learning and AI can give rise to a kind of general intelligence. This may be more than just a few years away, but it has been said that we are closer than many of us realise. Sam Harris, a neuroscientist, author and philosopher, outlines this in a succinct way by make the point that we are already on a continuum towards building a kind of general intelligence in our machines. He states that if super-intelligent AI is impossible, then something must be wrong with one of the three following assumptions.

Intelligence can simply arise in a physical system that is capable of information processing. As long as we continue to build better systems of atoms, we will eventually build one capable of general intelligence. Our brains have managed to do this and although complex, it is not beyond understanding.

The second is that we will keep developing better and better systems, improving on this previous version. As long as the human species exists, we will keep improving our systems. Nothing short of extinction could stop us.

The last assumption is that we, as humans, do not represent the peak of intelligence. If we were to build a self-learning AI that were as smart as we were, the idea is that it could improve upon itself and exceed us in ways we cannot imagine. Electronic circuits process information at speeds ridiculously faster than biological ones, so a general AI capable of self-learning would exceed us immediately.

Machine learning can give rise to an intelligence that would make machines superior to humans at just about any task. In terms of labour reduction, a super-intelligent AI capable of learning at an incredible rate would be revolutionary in its impact. People would be out of work due to not being able to keep up with machines. A general intelligence in a machine would have far too many advantages. One advantage includes the cost. A large lump sum for the technology itself would make for a costly investment, but a worthwhile one. This initial cost would be eclipsed over time as a machine does not require a wage like a human does. The costs involved would only include the initial sum, energy to power the machine and any repair costs. This kind of advantage already makes intelligent machines an attractive candidate for replacing employees, and yet there are many more advantages. Fair work would no longer be a concern - a machine cannot be injured or overworked (assuming proper maintenance of the machine, like cooling, oil, etc.). It does not require time off work, it does not need to be trained further than the initial machine learning process. Even so, the machine would learn the task at hand much faster than a human and beyond that, the same algorithm could then be applied to the rest machines in the same network. Machines are more reliable than humans - they are not late, they are not influenced by emotions or thoughts that are not relevant to the task at hand. Machines are capable of being precise right down to the smallest of units; a single millimetre, pixel, bit, particle, etc.

This would have a huge impact on everyone around the world. Due to the number of jobs being taken by machines, people would have no income to support themselves. The owners of the companies who built these machines (likely to a tech giant such as Google, Facebook, Tencent, etc.) would have the lion's share of the wealth in the world while the rest would starve. The presence of this level of AI would remove any need for any human labour. It would also remove the need for any human intellectual work. With this kind of intelligence available, it would solve problems that humans have been trying to solve for centuries. A cure for cancer, a solution to climate change, financial models being created in ways that we could never conceive on our own. To create an AI capable of general intelligence is to do so much more that just that - it provides an answer to many of the world's problems.
